Selected Strategic Work

Mixed 1981: Auckland CBD Café

Sample Engagement
Mixed 1981 is a Korean-inspired café in Auckland CBD with a genuinely rare "blue ocean" advantage: extended late-night hours and high-quality seating, something almost no other café in the area offers. I was brought in to figure out why that advantage wasn't translating into foot traffic or brand recognition.
This engagement is currently paused before execution, shown here as a full sample of the diagnostic, strategy, and action plan I deliver, not as a completed results case study. This is one example of the process, and the same framework applies well beyond cafés: any small business sitting on an advantage customers don't fully see yet.
01The Problem: An "Identity Gap"

Mixed 1981 had the ingredients for a standout brand, but the public perception didn't match the opportunity. The brand was sitting between four different identities at once: study café, dessert café, Korean-inspired café, and late-night café. That created confusion and weak recall instead of a clear reason to choose it. This is exactly the kind of gap that shows up on a Google Maps listing: what shows up in search doesn't match what the business actually is.

02How This Maps to a Google Maps Growth Plan

The diagnostic above isn't a generic brand exercise. Every finding feeds directly into the same three pillars below the services on this page.

Pillar 01

Profile & Positioning Reset

The positioning consult comes first, since a rewritten Google Business Profile description is only as good as the identity behind it.

  • Target persona: the "Ambitious Creative" and the "Deep-Work Student," the audience the rewritten description and categories would be built around.
  • Positioning line: "Seoul-Inspired Urban Retreat," short enough to open the Google description and anchor every category and attribute choice.
Google Maps audit actions
Eliminate confusion
  • Remove outdated photos from the café's former identity that dilute the premium Seoul aesthetic
  • Upload a high-density photo set so the first five images a searcher sees are current, high-quality, and on-brand
Social proof direction
What the photo shot list prioritizes
  • From photos of coffee to photos of people in their element: laptops, notebooks, and signature drinks in a focused setting
  • Makes the night-time usage visible and aspirational to anyone scrolling the listing before visiting
  • Their existing Instagram currently swings between warm, yellow-toned daytime shots and cool blue night shots with no consistent edit. One grading preset per mode (Day, Focus) would tie the Instagram grid and the Google listing together at a glance
Pillar 02

Monthly Maps Growth

Being open late is a function. A monthly content engine is what turns it into something worth posting about, on Google and Instagram alike.

Sample Google Posts content
The "Sunset Switch" angle

Two draft posts, built around a specific, searchable detail rather than a mood-board adjective:

Google Post, eveningThe lights go down, the focus goes up. Open till late for anyone chasing a deadline or a change of scenery. Try the Midnight Fuel oat latte and claim your corner.
Google Post, featureNew after 6pm: our caffeine-free Night-Cap barley tea, made in-house. For the after-dark crowd who still want something warm in hand.
Cross-platform consistency
Instagram: the "Rule of Two"
  • Two fonts locked in: a soft serif for evening captions and quotes, a clean sans for hours, menu, and info, so the voice reads the same whether someone lands on Instagram or a Google Post
  • A fixed weekly rhythm: Monday product (the Night-Cap drink), Wednesday person (a customer mid-focus, tagged with permission), Friday space (the Focus Mode lighting change), reused directly as the Pillar 02 monthly content calendar
Pillar 03

Local Collab Growth

This wasn't part of the original engagement, the collab concept came together after this diagnostic. But the same logic applies directly to a business like this. The Tea Thief, an Auckland boutique tea maker based on the North Shore and founded by Ruth Wynne-Capstick and David Capstick, already sells its organic, ethically-sourced blends under categories like "Focus" and "Caffeine Free," born out of Ruth's own search for a caffeine-free alternative during a health journey. That's a genuine, pre-existing product fit for a "Night-Cap" drink built specifically for Mixed 1981's Focus Mode hours, not a stretch pairing invented for a pitch deck. To be clear, this is illustrative, not an existing partnership, but it shows what a well-matched collab actually looks like: a maker whose own story already lines up with the moment in the customer's evening, rather than any nearby business with a product to move. The tea brand gets a curated space to introduce its blends to a new audience; Mixed 1981 gets a menu item with its own story to put in a Google Post or an Instagram caption. And a customer who tries something new and names it specifically in a review, "The Tea Thief's Night-Cap blend," is worth more to a Google listing than a generic five-star rating. That kind of specific, keyword-carrying detail is what actually helps a profile surface for the exact searches that matter.
